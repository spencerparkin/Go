The rules of Go

Go is a game of simple rules that leads to complex consequences and strategies. Many introductions to the game of Go make things too complicated by focusing on such complexities in the midst of these rules. We will not make the same mistake here.

The basic definitions and rules of Go are as follows.

  1. The board begins blank. Two players take turns placing one and only one stone of their color on a blank intersection of their choice. The intersections are marked by where the lines cross each other on the board. Intersections also occur on the corners and edges of the board. Typical board sizes are 9x9, 13x13 or 19x19.
  2. Two intersections on the board (either one occupied by a stone or not) are considered adjacent if a line on the board immediately connects them without any intermediate intersection. (Two intersections are never diagonally adjacent.)
  3. For any two stones of the same color where a path can be traced from one to the other along adjacencies (marked by the lines on the board) where at each step a stone is encountered of the same color, they are considered to be in or part of the same collection referred to as a group. Note that a single stone can form its own group.
  4. The liberties of a group are the vacant intersections adjacent to that group. An intersection is adjacent to a group if it is adjacent to any stone in that group.
  5. If a placement made by one player removes all liberties of a group owned by the other, then the stones of that group are captured by the placing player. They are taken off the board and placed in the custody of the capturing player.
  6. You may not place a stone where it would create a group of your own color with no liberties. However, the number of liberties of the group containing the placed stone is determined after all capturing caused by the new placement has occurred.
  7. You may not place a stone where it would regenerate the entire state of the playing board that was experienced when it was last your opponent's turn. However, this board state can be repeated if it occurred further back in the history of the game.
  8. For any two vacant intersections where a path can be traced from one to the other along adjacencies (marked by the lines on the board) where at each step a vacant intersection is encountered, they are considered to be in or part of the same collection referred to as a territory. The amount of territory is the sum of vacant intersections making up the territory.
  9. If all occupied adjacencies of a territory are of the same color, then the player of that color owns the territory; otherwise, no one owns the territory.
  10. At any point during the course of the game, a player's score is calculated as the sum of the amounts of all the territories he/she owns, minus the number of stones his/her opponent has captured.
  11. Either player may choose to forfeit their turn. The game has ended when either both players do this consecutively, or both agree that neither has anything to gain by continuing the game. Finally, the player with the highest score at the end of the game wins.

And there we have it. That is the game of Go, as I understand it, and put in the simplest terms that I can muster. That withstanding, I'll now proceed to clarify some of what's been said with a few examples.

Example 1

Consider the following example 3x3 Go board.

Here, the white stone on bottom is adjacent to the black stone, but the white stone on top is not adjacent to the black stone. The black stone forms its own group having two liberties, the vacant intersections above and below the stone. The two white stones form a group having four liberties. Notice that if black placed a stone at the bottom middle intersection, then that player would gain the territory consisting of the single vacant intersection in the bottom-right corner of the board.

Example 2

Now consider another example; this time on a 5x5 Go board.

In this case, it is white's turn. At first glance, it might seem against the rules to place a white stone in the territory captured by the four black stones and consisting of a single vacant intersection on the board. (One might think that the placed stone would be immediately captured.) It is a legal move, however, because white first captures the black stone at the board's center before we consider the liberties of the placed stone. Making this move, the board then becomes as follows.

After capturing a black stone, white now owns a new territory consisting of the vacant intersection at the board's center. Being now black's turn, however, he cannot make the same kind of move that white just did by capturing the white stone just placed by white. This is because it would repeat a state of the board too soon. A turn or more later, however, if there is still the opportunity, black could then make that placement.

The two territories owned by white here are called eyes of the white group. Eyes like this make the group impervious to capture. If the eyes were larger, the group would not necessarily be invincible, although having two or more eyes is still a very desirable trait for any group, because it makes the group nearly invulnerable to capture. A fight for the territory in an eye will reducie its size, and in the end, the group may end up with two or more eyes as above, making the group truely invulnerable to capture at that point. Note that the territory currently owned by white above is incontestable.

Going further with Go

The material covered thus far, I believe, completes the minimum requirements needed to begin playing the game. Though I myself am nothing more than an amature Go player, it should at least be mentioned here that the consequences of the basic rules of Go lead to a great deal of complexity and stratagy that many, over the thousands of years that Go has been played, have discovered and documented. A serious Go player must seek out advanced material on the subject of Go if they want to take the game further. Until I learn more about the game, this is where I'm going to have to end this tutorial. In any case, just have fun with it!
